Chapter 1 · Adverbs
Adverbs
Words that modify verbs and adjectives
1.1
Negation of "you" with "mei" — 没 + 有
没有
méiyǒu
Pattern没 + 有 (+ Object)

没 (méi) is used specifically to negate 有 (yǒu — to have). You cannot use 不有 — it is always 没有. In casual speech, 有 is often dropped and just 没 is used alone. This is one of the most important negation patterns at A1 level.

Examples
我没有钱。
Wǒ méiyǒu qián.
I don't have money.
他没有时间。
Tā méiyǒu shíjiān.
He doesn't have time.
我没有哥哥。
Wǒ méiyǒu gēge.
I don't have an older brother.
你有没有问题？
Nǐ yǒu méiyǒu wèntí?
Do you have questions? (affirmative-negative form)
⚠️NEVER say 不有. Always use 没有 to negate 有. In speech, 没有 can be shortened to just 没: 我没钱 (I have no money).
1.2
Standard Negation with "bu" — 不 + Verb / Adj.
不
bù / bú
Pattern不 + Verb / Adjective

不 (bù) is the standard negation word in Chinese, placed directly before a verb or adjective. Its tone changes to 2nd tone (bú) before a 4th-tone syllable. It covers most present/future negation. The one exception is 有 (have), which uses 没 instead.

Examples
他们不是坏孩子。
Tāmen bú shì huài háizi.
They are not bad children.
我不喝酒。
Wǒ bù hē jiǔ.
I don't drink alcohol.
今天不冷。
Jīntiān bù lěng.
Today is not cold.
她不去。
Tā bù qù.
She is not going.
💡Tone rule: 不 is 4th tone (bù) by default, but becomes 2nd tone (bú) before another 4th-tone syllable: 不是 (bú shì), 不去 (bú qù).
1.3
The "All" Adverb "dou" — 都 + Verb / Adj.
都
dōu
PatternPlural Subject + 都 + Verb / Adj.

都 (dōu) means "all" or "both" and always refers back to a plural subject. It must come before the verb or adjective, never at the end. When combined with 不, 都不 means "none of them" while 不都 means "not all of them."

Examples
我们都住在上海。
Wǒmen dōu zhù zài Shànghǎi.
We all live in Shanghai.
他们都是学生。
Tāmen dōu shì xuésheng.
They are all students.
我和他都喜欢音乐。
Wǒ hé tā dōu xǐhuān yīnyuè.
Both he and I like music.
这些菜都很好吃。
Zhèxiē cài dōu hěn hǎochī.
All these dishes are delicious.
💡都不 = none of them (e.g. 他们都不去 — none of them are going). 不都 = not all of them (some go, some don't).
1.4
The "Also" Adverb "ye" — 也 + Verb / Adj.
也
yě
PatternSubject + 也 + Verb / Adj.

也 (yě) means "also" or "too" and is placed before the verb or adjective — never at the end of the sentence like English "too." It must come after the subject and before the predicate.

Examples
昨天很冷，今天也很冷。
Zuótiān hěn lěng, jīntiān yě hěn lěng.
Yesterday was cold; today is also cold.
我也是学生。
Wǒ yě shì xuésheng.
I am also a student.
他也去。
Tā yě qù.
He is going too.
她也很高。
Tā yě hěn gāo.
She is also very tall.
⚠️也 must come BEFORE the verb. "我学中文也" is incorrect. The correct form is "我也学中文."
Chapter 2 · Conjunctions
Conjunctions
Connecting words and ideas
2.1
Expressing "And" with "he" — Noun 1 + 和 + Noun 2
和
hé
PatternNoun 1 + 和 + Noun 2

和 (hé) connects nouns or pronouns, meaning "and." It is only used to link nouns — not verbs or clauses. For connecting actions in sequence, use 然后 (ránhòu, "then/afterwards") instead.

Examples
我和他都不去。
Wǒ hé tā dōu bù qù.
He and I are both not going.
茶和咖啡
chá hé kāfēi
tea and coffee
妈妈和爸爸
māma hé bàba
mom and dad
中文和英文都很难。
Zhōngwén hé Yīngwén dōu hěn nán.
Both Chinese and English are difficult.
💡和 only connects NOUNS. To link actions: 我去学校，然后学习 (I go to school, then study). Don't use 和 between verbs.
2.2
Offering Choices with "haishi" — A + 还是 + B?
还是
háishi
PatternA + 还是 + B ?

还是 (háishi) means "or" in questions that offer a choice between two options. It is only used in questions. In statements (non-questions), the word for "or" is 或者 (huòzhě). Do not mix them up.

Examples
你要喝茶还是咖啡？
Nǐ yào hē chá háishi kāfēi?
Do you want tea or coffee?
你是学生还是老师？
Nǐ shì xuésheng háishi lǎoshī?
Are you a student or a teacher?
你去还是不去？
Nǐ qù háishi bù qù?
Are you going or not?
你喜欢猫还是狗？
Nǐ xǐhuān māo háishi gǒu?
Do you like cats or dogs?
⚠️还是 = "or" in QUESTIONS. 或者 = "or" in STATEMENTS. Wrong: 你可以喝茶还是咖啡 (statement). Right: 你可以喝茶或者咖啡.
Chapter 3 · Numbers
Numbers, Time & Dates
Counting, telling time, and expressing dates
3.1
Age with "sui" — Subj. + Number + 岁
岁
suì
PatternSubject + Number + 岁 (+ 了)

To state someone's age, simply place the number before 岁. No verb like 是 is needed — 岁 acts as its own predicate. To ask age: use 几岁? (jǐ suì?) for young children and 多大? (duō dà?) for adults and teens.

Examples
我20岁。
Wǒ èrshí suì.
I am 20 years old.
他三岁。
Tā sān suì.
He is three years old.
你多大？
Nǐ duō dà?
How old are you? (to an adult)
她今年三十岁了。
Tā jīnnián sānshí suì le.
She is turning 30 this year.
💡Ask children: 你几岁? Ask adults/teens: 你多大? The 了 at the end of age sentences signals a new state — she is now 30.
3.2
Measure Word "ge" — Number + 个 + Noun
个
gè
PatternNumber + 个 + Noun

Chinese requires a measure word between a number and a noun — you cannot say "three person" directly. 个 (gè) is the most versatile and most commonly used measure word, suitable for people and many objects when you're unsure which specific measure word to use.

Examples
一个人
yī gè rén
one person
两个学生
liǎng gè xuésheng
two students
三个问题
sān gè wèntí
three questions
这个苹果很甜。
Zhège píngguǒ hěn tián.
This apple is very sweet.
💡Other common measure words: 本 (běn, books), 张 (zhāng, flat things), 杯 (bēi, cups), 只 (zhī, small animals), 件 (jiàn, clothing). When unsure, 个 usually works.
3.3
Structure of Dates — Number + 年 + Number + 月 + Number + 日
年 月 日／号
nián, yuè, rì / hào
PatternYear + 年 + Month + 月 + Day + 日/号

Dates in Chinese go from largest to smallest: year → month → day. This is the opposite of English (month/day/year). 日 (rì) is formal; 号 (hào) is the everyday spoken form for the day of the month. The year is always read digit by digit.

Examples
今天是2011年11月11日。
Jīntiān shì èr líng yī yī nián shíyī yuè shíyī hào.
Today is November 11, 2011.
我的生日是三月五号。
Wǒ de shēngrì shì sān yuè wǔ hào.
My birthday is March 5th.
今天几号？
Jīntiān jǐ hào?
What is today's date?
现在几月？
Xiànzài jǐ yuè?
What month is it now?
💡Months: 一月 (January) through 十二月 (December). Years are read digit by digit: 2025 = 二零二五年 (èr líng èr wǔ nián).
3.4
Structure of Days of the Week — 星期 + Number
星期
xīngqī
Pattern星期 + Number (1–6) / 天 or 日

Days of the week are formed by adding a number to 星期 (xīngqī). Monday is 星期一 (1), through Saturday 星期六 (6). Sunday is the exception: 星期天 or 星期日. To ask what day it is: 今天星期几？

Examples
今天星期三。
Jīntiān xīngqīsān.
Today is Wednesday.
星期一 / 星期二 / 星期三
xīngqīyī / xīngqīèr / xīngqīsān
Monday / Tuesday / Wednesday
星期四 / 星期五 / 星期六
xīngqīsì / xīngqīwǔ / xīngqīliù
Thursday / Friday / Saturday
星期天 / 星期日
xīngqītiān / xīngqīrì
Sunday
💡周 (zhōu) is an alternative to 星期: 周一, 周二… 周日. Both are correct and equally common. 周 is slightly more formal/written.
3.5
Structure of Numbers — Number + Unit
百 千 万
bǎi, qiān, wàn
PatternNumber + Unit (百/千/万)

Chinese numbers are very logical. Units: 十 (10), 百 (100), 千 (1,000), 万 (10,000). Numbers are formed by combining these units. Note: Chinese has a special unit 万 for 10,000 — there is no single word for 10,000 in English, but Chinese uses it constantly.

Examples
三百五十九
sān bǎi wǔshíjiǔ
359
一千零八十
yī qiān líng bāshí
1,080 (零 fills gaps)
两万五千
liǎng wàn wǔqiān
25,000
一百万
yī bǎi wàn
1,000,000 (one million)
💡Use 零 (líng) when there is a zero in the middle of a number: 一百零五 (105). The zero is spoken but a leading zero is omitted: 1,005 = 一千零五.
3.6
Structure of Times (Basic) — Number + 点(钟)
点 分
diǎn, fēn
PatternNumber + 点(钟) (+ Number + 分)

To tell the time, use the hour number + 点 (diǎn). Add minutes with 分 (fēn). 半 (bàn) means "half past." 钟 (zhōng) is optional after the hour alone. To ask the time: 现在几点？(What time is it now?)

Examples
现在十一点。
Xiànzài shíyī diǎn.
It is now 11 o'clock.
三点半
sān diǎn bàn
3:30 (half past three)
八点十五分
bā diǎn shíwǔ fēn
8:15
你几点来？
Nǐ jǐ diǎn lái?
What time are you coming?
💡AM/PM: 上午 (shàngwǔ) = morning, 下午 (xiàwǔ) = afternoon, 晚上 (wǎnshang) = evening. Place before the time: 下午三点 (3 PM).
3.7
Comparing "er" and "liang" — 二 vs 两
二 vs 两
èr vs liǎng
Pattern两 + Measure Word + Noun  |  二 + Unit (十/百…)

Both 二 and 两 mean "two," but they are used in different contexts. 两 is used before measure words and units of measurement. 二 is used in sequences (numbers, floors, bus routes) and in numbers like 12, 20, 22. This is one of the most common beginner mistakes.

Examples
两个人
liǎng gè rén
two people (两 before measure word)
二十二
èrshíèr
22 (二 in sequences)
两本书
liǎng běn shū
two books (两 before measure word)
第二 / 二楼
dì èr / èr lóu
second / second floor (序数 uses 二)
⚠️Before measure words → always 两: 两个 ✓, 二个 ✗. In compound numbers → 二: 十二 (12), 二十 (20). Two hundred = 两百 OR 二百 (both OK).
Chapter 4 · Particles
Particles
Small words that carry big grammatical meaning
4.1
Expressing Close Possession without "de" — Pronoun + Noun
我女朋友
wǒ nǚpéngyou
PatternPronoun + Noun (no 的)

When showing possession of people in close personal relationships — family members, romantic partners, close friends — 的 (de) is usually dropped. Including 的 in these cases sounds overly formal or creates emotional distance.

Examples
我女朋友
wǒ nǚpéngyou
my girlfriend
我妈妈
wǒ māma
my mom
我朋友
wǒ péngyou
my friend
他哥哥
tā gēge
his older brother
💡For things (not people), keep 的: 我的书 (my book), 我的手机 (my phone). Drop 的 mainly for close personal relationships.
4.2
Expressing Possession with "de" — Noun 1 + 的 + Noun 2
的
de
PatternPossessor + 的 + Thing possessed

的 (de) is the possessive particle in Chinese, equivalent to 's or "of" in English. It comes between the possessor and what is possessed. It also links a modifier to a noun (e.g. adjective + 的 + noun).

Examples
我的老师
wǒ de lǎoshī
my teacher
他的名字
tā de míngzi
his name
中国的文化
Zhōngguó de wénhuà
China's culture
这是我的。
Zhè shì wǒ de.
This is mine.
💡的 can stand alone at the end of a sentence to replace the noun: 这是我的 (This is mine). The noun is implied from context.
4.3
Questions with "ne" — ……呢？
呢
ne
PatternStatement. + Noun/Pronoun + 呢？

呢 (ne) is added after a noun or pronoun to ask "And what about…?" It echoes the previous question or topic back, creating natural conversation flow without needing to repeat the full question.

Examples
我很好。你呢？
Wǒ hěn hǎo. Nǐ ne?
I'm fine. And you?
他去了，她呢？
Tā qù le, tā ne?
He went; what about her?
我喜欢，你呢？
Wǒ xǐhuān, nǐ ne?
I like it; what about you?
这个我知道，那个呢？
Zhège wǒ zhīdào, nàge ne?
I know this one; what about that one?
💡呢 mid-sentence can also indicate ongoing action: 他在睡觉呢 (He is sleeping right now). Context makes clear which usage applies.
4.4
Suggestions with "ba" — Command + 吧
吧
ba
PatternStatement / Command + 吧

吧 (ba) softens a command into a suggestion, expresses an assumption, or seeks confirmation. It makes the tone relaxed and friendly. It can mean "Let's…", "Right?", or "I assume…" depending on context.

Examples
我们走吧。
Wǒmen zǒu ba.
Let's go.
你累了吧？
Nǐ lèi le ba?
You must be tired, right?
喝点水吧。
Hē diǎn shuǐ ba.
Have some water.
是这样吧？
Shì zhèyàng ba?
It's like this, right?
💡吧 turns any statement into a gentle suggestion or soft assumption. It always softens the tone — never adds aggression or urgency.
Chapter 5 · Verbs
Verbs
Actions, states, and existence
5.1
Expressing Existence in a Place with "zai" — 在 + Place
在
zài
PatternSubject + 在 + Place

在 (zài) as a verb means "to be at/in/on (a location)." It expresses where someone or something is located. It can also be used as a preposition before an action verb to indicate where an action takes place.

Examples
老师在上海。
Lǎoshī zài Shànghǎi.
The teacher is in Shanghai.
我在家。
Wǒ zài jiā.
I am at home.
书在桌子上。
Shū zài zhuōzi shàng.
The book is on the table.
你在哪里？
Nǐ zài nǎlǐ?
Where are you?
💡Location words: 上 (on), 下 (under), 里 (inside), 旁边 (beside), 前面 (in front), 后面 (behind), 左边 (left), 右边 (right).
5.2
Expressing Existence with "you" — Place + 有 + Obj.
有
yǒu
PatternPlace + 有 + Object

有 (yǒu) can express existence, equivalent to "there is/are." In this usage, the sentence starts with a place and ends with the thing that exists there. This is different from the possessive use of 有 (GP 5.3), where the subject is a person.

Examples
我们学校有很多学生。
Wǒmen xuéxiào yǒu hěn duō xuésheng.
Our school has a lot of students. / There are many students at our school.
这里有一家咖啡店。
Zhèlǐ yǒu yī jiā kāfēidiàn.
There is a coffee shop here.
附近有没有银行？
Fùjìn yǒu méiyǒu yínháng?
Is there a bank nearby?
冰箱里有什么？
Bīngxiāng lǐ yǒu shénme?
What is in the fridge?
💡Existence structure: Place + 有 + Thing. Possession structure: Person + 有 + Thing. The verb 有 is the same; the subject tells you which meaning applies.
5.3
Expressing Possession with "you" — Subj. + 有 + Obj.
有
yǒu
PatternSubject + 有 + Object

有 (yǒu) means "to have" when the subject is a person or entity possessing something. Negate with 没有 (méiyǒu) — never 不有. This is one of the most frequently used verbs in Mandarin.

Examples
我有钱。
Wǒ yǒu qián.
I have money.
他有一个哥哥。
Tā yǒu yī gè gēge.
He has an older brother.
你有问题吗？
Nǐ yǒu wèntí ma?
Do you have questions?
我没有时间。
Wǒ méiyǒu shíjiān.
I don't have time.
⚠️NEVER say 不有. Negate 有 with 没有 only. In speech, 没有 → 没: 我没钱 (I have no money).
5.4
Using the Verb "jiao" — 叫 + Name
叫
jiào
PatternSubject + 叫 + Name / Noun

叫 (jiào) means "to be called" or "to call." The most common use for beginners is to introduce your name: 我叫… (My name is… / I am called…). It is more casual than 我的名字是 (My name is). Ask with 你叫什么名字？

Examples
你叫什么名字？
Nǐ jiào shénme míngzi?
What is your name?
我叫李明。
Wǒ jiào Lǐ Míng.
My name is Li Ming.
她叫什么？
Tā jiào shénme?
What is her name?
这个叫饺子。
Zhège jiào jiǎozi.
This is called dumplings.
💡叫 also means "to call/shout": 你叫他来 (Call him to come). Context makes clear which meaning is intended.
5.5
Using the Verb "qu" — 去 + Place
去
qù
PatternSubject + 去 + Place / Verb

去 (qù) means "to go" (away from the speaker's current location). It can be followed directly by a place, or by another verb phrase to mean "go to do something." It is one of the highest-frequency verbs in Mandarin.

Examples
我去学校。
Wǒ qù xuéxiào.
I go to school.
你去哪儿？
Nǐ qù nǎr?
Where are you going?
我要去吃饭，你去吗？
Wǒ yào qù chīfàn, nǐ qù ma?
I'm going to eat; are you coming?
她去超市买东西。
Tā qù chāoshì mǎi dōngxi.
She goes to the supermarket to buy things.
💡去 + Verb = go to do something: 去吃饭 (go eat), 去买东西 (go shopping), 去看朋友 (go see a friend). Very natural and common structure.
5.6
Using the Verb "xing" — 姓 + Surname
姓
xìng
PatternSubject + 姓 + Surname

姓 (xìng) means "to have the family name of" or "my surname is." It is specifically used for family names (surnames), not full names. To ask someone's surname politely: 您贵姓？(What is your honorable surname?) or informally: 你姓什么？

Examples
你姓什么？
Nǐ xìng shénme?
What is your surname?
我姓王。
Wǒ xìng Wáng.
My surname is Wang.
他姓李。
Tā xìng Lǐ.
His surname is Li.
您贵姓？
Nín guì xìng?
What is your honorable surname? (formal/polite)
💡姓 is for surnames only. Use 叫 for full names: 我叫王明 (My name is Wang Ming). In China, surnames come first: 王 (family name) 明 (given name).
Chapter 6 · Auxiliary Verbs
Auxiliary Verbs
Modal verbs that modify the main verb
6.1
Expressing Ability or Possibility with "neng" — 能 + Verb
能
néng
PatternSubject + 能 + Verb

能 (néng) expresses physical ability or situational possibility — whether someone is able to do something given circumstances. It is different from 会 (learned skill) and 可以 (permission). Negate with 不能.

Examples
中文这么难，你能学好吗？
Zhōngwén zhème nán, nǐ néng xué hǎo ma?
Chinese is so hard — are you able to learn it well?
我今天不能来。
Wǒ jīntiān bù néng lái.
I can't come today (circumstances prevent it).
这里不能抽烟。
Zhèlǐ bù néng chōuyān.
You cannot smoke here.
你能帮我吗？
Nǐ néng bāng wǒ ma?
Are you able to help me?
💡能 (circumstances) vs 会 (learned skill) vs 可以 (permission). "Can you speak Chinese?" — 你会说中文吗? (learned skill) / 你能说中文吗? (are you able to right now?)
6.2
Expressing a Learned Skill with "hui" — 会 + Verb
会
huì
PatternSubject + 会 + Verb

会 (huì) expresses a skill that was learned through study or practice — "can" in the sense of "know how to." It is the correct choice when talking about language ability, sports, musical instruments, and other acquired skills. Negate with 不会.

Examples
他会说中文。
Tā huì shuō Zhōngwén.
He can speak Chinese. (learned skill)
我会游泳。
Wǒ huì yóuyǒng.
I can swim. (know how to)
你会开车吗？
Nǐ huì kāi chē ma?
Can you drive? (do you know how?)
我不会弹钢琴。
Wǒ bù huì tán gāngqín.
I can't play the piano. (never learned)
💡Use 会 for skills you've learned: languages, sports, instruments, cooking. Use 能 for what you're physically or situationally able to do right now.
6.3
Expressing "Be Going to" with "yao" — Subj. + 要 + Verb (+ 了)
要……了
yào … le
PatternSubject + 要 + Verb (+ 了)

要 (yào) with 了 at the end expresses that something is about to happen — "is going to" or "will soon." The 了 signals an imminent change of state. Without 了, 要 simply means "want to" (see GP 6.5).

Examples
我要去吃饭，你去吗？
Wǒ yào qù chīfàn, nǐ qù ma?
I'm going to go eat — are you coming?
快要下雨了。
Kuài yào xià yǔ le.
It's about to rain.
他要来了。
Tā yào lái le.
He is about to arrive.
我们要出发了。
Wǒmen yào chūfā le.
We are about to set off.
💡快要…了 (kuài yào…le) = "about to / on the verge of": 快要下课了 (Class is about to end). 快 adds the sense of "very soon."
6.4
Expressing Permission with "keyi" — 可以 + Verb
可以
kěyǐ
PatternSubject + 可以 + Verb

可以 (kěyǐ) expresses permission — "may" or "is allowed to." It is the most polite way to ask for or grant permission. To deny permission, use 不可以 or 不能. 可以 is also commonly used to make polite requests.

Examples
这里可以吸烟吗？
Zhèlǐ kěyǐ xīyān ma?
Is smoking allowed here?
你可以进来。
Nǐ kěyǐ jìnlái.
You may come in.
我可以问一个问题吗？
Wǒ kěyǐ wèn yī gè wèntí ma?
May I ask a question?
这里不可以拍照。
Zhèlǐ bù kěyǐ pāizhào.
Photography is not allowed here.
💡可以 (permission) vs 能 (ability/circumstance): 我可以用你的手机吗？(May I use your phone? — permission) vs 你能听到吗？(Can you hear? — ability)
6.5
Wanting to Do Something with "yao" — 要 + Verb
要
yào
PatternSubject + 要 + Verb

要 (yào) without 了 expresses a strong desire or intention to do something — "want to" or "need to." It is stronger and more definite than 想 (which is softer: "would like to"). 要 can also be used to order something (at a restaurant, shop).

Examples
我要吃饭。
Wǒ yào chīfàn.
I want to eat.
我要一杯咖啡。
Wǒ yào yī bēi kāfēi.
I want a cup of coffee.
他要去北京。
Tā yào qù Běijīng.
He wants to go to Beijing.
你要什么？
Nǐ yào shénme?
What do you want?
💡想 (xiǎng) = "would like to" (softer, polite). 要 (yào) = "want/will" (stronger, more determined). In a restaurant: 我要... is the standard way to order.
Chapter 7 · Verb Phrases
Verb Phrases
Multi-word expressions built around verbs
7.1
How to Do Something with "zenme" — 怎么 + Verb?
怎么
zěnme
Pattern怎么 + Verb?

怎么 (zěnme) means "how" and is placed directly before the verb it modifies. Unlike English, the question word stays in the position where the answer goes — it does not move to the front. 怎么 asks about the manner or method of doing something.

Examples
怎么走？
Zěnme zǒu?
How do you get there? / Which way?
这个字怎么写？
Zhège zì zěnme xiě?
How do you write this character?
这个怎么说？
Zhège zěnme shuō?
How do you say this?
你怎么去学校？
Nǐ zěnme qù xuéxiào?
How do you go to school?
💡怎么 (how — manner) vs 怎么样 (zěnmeyàng — how is it? / how about it?). 你怎么样？means "How are you?" / "How's it going?"
7.2
Negation of Past Actions with "meiyou" — 没有 + Verb
没有
méiyǒu
PatternSubject + 没有 + Verb

To negate a past action (something that did not happen), use 没有 before the verb. Do not use 不 for past negation of actions. 没有 indicates the action was not completed or did not occur. It can be shortened to 没 in casual speech.

Examples
我没有去酒吧。
Wǒ méiyǒu qù jiǔbā.
I didn't go to the bar.
他没有来。
Tā méiyǒu lái.
He didn't come.
我没有吃早饭。
Wǒ méiyǒu chī zǎofàn.
I didn't eat breakfast.
你去了吗？没有。
Nǐ qù le ma? Méiyǒu.
Did you go? No (I didn't).
⚠️Past negation: 没有 + Verb ✓   不 + Verb ✗ (for past). "我不去了" means "I'm no longer going" (present change), not "I didn't go."
7.3
Negative Commands with "buyao" — 不要 + Verb
不要
bù yào
Pattern不要 + Verb

不要 (bù yào) or its shortened form 别 (bié) is used to give negative commands — telling someone "don't" do something. 别 is more casual and common in speech. Both come directly before the verb with no subject needed (imperative).

Examples
不要说英文！
Bù yào shuō Yīngwén!
Don't speak English!
别说话！
Bié shuōhuà!
Don't talk! (casual)
不要担心。
Bù yào dānxīn.
Don't worry.
别忘了！
Bié wàng le!
Don't forget!
💡不要 and 别 are interchangeable in most cases. 别 is more spoken/casual. Signs and formal notices tend to use 不要 or 请勿 (qǐng wù — please do not).
Chapter 8 · Grammatical Structures: Basics
Basic Grammatical Structures
Core sentence patterns that underpin all of Mandarin
8.1
Basic Sentence Order — Subj. + Verb (+ Obj.)
主 + 谓 + 宾
zhǔ + wèi + bīn
PatternSubject + Verb (+ Object)

Chinese follows Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order like English. However, time and place expressions come before the verb (not after), and adverbs also go before the verb. The object can be omitted when clear from context.

Examples
我爱你。
Wǒ ài nǐ.
I love you. (S + V + O)
我明天去北京。
Wǒ míngtiān qù Běijīng.
I am going to Beijing tomorrow. (time before verb)
他在家吃饭。
Tā zài jiā chīfàn.
He eats at home. (place before verb)
我每天学习。
Wǒ měitiān xuéxí.
I study every day.
💡Key difference from English: time words (今天, 明天, 每天) go BEFORE the verb in Chinese, not after. "I go tomorrow" → 我明天去, not 我去明天.
8.2
Connecting Nouns with "shi" — A + 是 + B
是
shì
PatternA + 是 + B

是 (shì) links two nouns or noun phrases — "A is B." It equates or identifies the subject. It is never used before adjectives (use 很 instead). Negated with 不是 (bú shì). Questions: add 吗 at the end, or use the 是不是 form.

Examples
我是学生。
Wǒ shì xuésheng.
I am a student.
他是我的老师。
Tā shì wǒ de lǎoshī.
He is my teacher.
这是什么？
Zhè shì shénme?
What is this?
他不是老板。
Tā bú shì lǎobǎn.
He is not the boss.
⚠️是 = noun-to-noun link. NEVER before adjectives: 我是累 ✗ → 我很累 ✓. 他是高 ✗ → 他很高 ✓.
8.3
Expressing "Excessively" with "tai" — 太 + Adj. + 了
太……了
tài … le
Pattern太 + Adjective + 了

太 (tài) means "too" or "excessively." It always pairs with 了 at the end of the sentence. Together they mean "too [adjective]" or can be used positively as an exclamation: 太好了！(Great! / Excellent!). The 了 is required — do not omit it.

Examples
太好了！
Tài hǎo le!
Excellent! / Great! / Too good!
这个太贵了。
Zhège tài guì le.
This is too expensive.
今天太热了！
Jīntiān tài rè le!
Today is too hot!
你说得太快了。
Nǐ shuō de tài kuài le.
You speak too fast.
💡太好了！is one of the most common Chinese exclamations, meaning "Great!" or "Wonderful!" — used very positively, not just "too good." Context determines whether it's a complaint or a compliment.
8.4
Simple "Noun + Adjective" Sentences — Noun + 很 + Adj.
我很好。
Wǒ hěn hǎo.
PatternNoun + 很 + Adjective

In Chinese, adjectives can function as predicates directly — no "to be" verb (是) is needed. 很 (hěn, "very") is placed before the adjective. When unstressed in normal speech, 很 does not actually mean "very" — it is a linking device. Without 很, a bare adjective implies comparison.

Examples
我很好。
Wǒ hěn hǎo.
I am (very) good / fine.
她很漂亮。
Tā hěn piàoliang.
She is (very) pretty.
今天很冷。
Jīntiān hěn lěng.
Today is (very) cold.
这个菜很好吃。
Zhège cài hěn hǎochī.
This dish is (very) delicious.
⚠️A bare adjective without 很 implies comparison: 她漂亮 sounds like "She is prettier (than someone)." Always include 很 for neutral, non-comparative statements.
Chapter 9 · Question Forms
Question Forms
All the ways to ask questions in Mandarin
9.1
Affirmative-Negative Question — Verb + 不 + Verb
去不去？好不好？
qù bù qù? hǎo bù hǎo?
PatternVerb + 不 + Verb  |  Adj. + 不 + Adj.

This form repeats the verb (or adjective) with 不 sandwiched in between, offering an affirmative-negative choice. It is a natural, casual way to ask yes/no questions and is equivalent to adding 吗 but often more conversational.

Examples
去不去？
Qù bù qù?
Going or not?
好不好？
Hǎo bù hǎo?
Good or not? / Is that OK?
你是不是学生？
Nǐ shì bú shì xuésheng?
Are you a student or not?
你吃不吃肉？
Nǐ chī bù chī ròu?
Do you eat meat or not?
💡For longer verbs, only the first syllable may be repeated: 喜不喜欢？(Do you like it?), 认不认识？(Do you know them?). This is very natural in speech.
9.2
Placement of Question Words — In Situ
什么 谁 哪儿 为什么 怎么
shénme, shéi, nǎr, wèishénme, zěnme
PatternQuestion word stays in the position of the answer

Chinese question words (who, what, where, when, why, how) do NOT move to the beginning of the sentence like in English. They stay exactly where the answer would appear. No 吗 is added when a question word is present.

Examples
你是谁？
Nǐ shì shéi?
Who are you? (谁 where the name would go)
你在哪儿？
Nǐ zài nǎr?
Where are you? (哪儿 where the place would go)
你要什么？
Nǐ yào shénme?
What do you want? (什么 where the object would go)
你为什么学中文？
Nǐ wèishénme xué Zhōngwén?
Why do you study Chinese?
💡All question words: 什么 (what), 谁 (who), 哪里/哪儿 (where), 什么时候 (when), 为什么 (why), 怎么 (how), 多少/几 (how many/much).
9.3
Tag Questions with "bu" — ……是不是 / 对不对 / 好不好?
是不是 对不对 好不好
shì bú shì, duì bú duì, hǎo bù hǎo
PatternStatement + 是不是 / 对不对 / 好不好?

Chinese tag questions using 不 are formed by appending a short affirmative-negative phrase at the end of a statement. Common tags: 是不是 (right or not?), 对不对 (correct or not?), 好不好 (good or not? — used to seek agreement).

Examples
你们明天来，好不好？
Nǐmen míngtiān lái, hǎo bù hǎo?
You're coming tomorrow, OK?
他是老师，对不对？
Tā shì lǎoshī, duì bú duì?
He's a teacher, right?
这样做，是不是？
Zhèyàng zuò, shì bú shì?
Do it this way, right?
我们先吃，好不好？
Wǒmen xiān chī, hǎo bù hǎo?
Let's eat first, OK?
💡好不好？at the end of a suggestion is like "How about it?" or "Deal?" — very commonly used to seek agreement on a proposal or plan.
9.4
Tag Questions with "ma" — ……是吗 / 对吗 / 好吗?
是吗 对吗 好吗
shì ma, duì ma, hǎo ma
PatternStatement + 是吗 / 对吗 / 好吗?

Tag questions with 吗 are formed by adding 是吗, 对吗, or 好吗 after a statement. They are similar to English "right?", "is that so?", or "OK?" They seek confirmation but are slightly softer than the 不 form. 是吗 can also express surprise: "Oh really?"

Examples
这样做，对吗？
Zhèyàng zuò, duì ma?
Doing it this way is correct, right?
你是老师，是吗？
Nǐ shì lǎoshī, shì ma?
You're a teacher, right?
他赢了？是吗！
Tā yíng le? Shì ma!
He won? Oh really!
我们明天见，好吗？
Wǒmen míngtiān jiàn, hǎo ma?
We'll meet tomorrow, OK?
💡是吗 (shì ma) has a double function: tag question ("right?") AND expression of surprise/disbelief ("Oh really? Is that so?"). Tone of voice distinguishes them.
9.5
Yes-No Questions with "ma" — ……吗？
……吗？
… ma?
PatternStatement + 吗？

The simplest way to form a yes/no question: add 吗 to any statement. Word order does not change — unlike English, there is no subject-verb inversion. To answer "yes," repeat the verb. To answer "no," use 不/没有 + verb.

Examples
他是老板吗？
Tā shì lǎobǎn ma?
Is he the boss?
你喜欢中国菜吗？
Nǐ xǐhuān Zhōngguó cài ma?
Do you like Chinese food?
你有时间吗？
Nǐ yǒu shíjiān ma?
Do you have time?
你去吗？去。/ 不去。
Nǐ qù ma? Qù. / Bù qù.
Are you going? Yes. / No.
💡To answer "yes": repeat the verb (去, 是, 有). To answer "no": 不 + verb or 没有 + verb. Never say 是 alone as "yes" in Chinese — it sounds unnatural for most questions.
